I am an empirical economist with research interests in urban economics, labour economics, and economic history. My research has touched on several subfields of economics, including migration, agglomeration of economics activity, peer effects, knowledge spillovers. I use novel, historical datasets to study the migration and geographic clustering of creative workers and to identify agglomeration effects associated with these creative clusters. My most recent research has also used these historical data to study women's labour force participation in creative industries.

I am currently a Postdoctoral Researcher at the Chair of Urban, Regional, and International Economics (URI) at TU Dortmund. I received a PhD in Economics from Trinity College Dublin (Ireland). I was one of the inaugural Grattan Scholars at Trinity College Dublin, and I was selected as an Irish Research Council Fellow at the 5th Lindau Nobel Laureate Meeting on Economic Sciences. I received my MSc in Economics and MPhil in International Peace Studies from Trinity College Dublin (Ireland), and a BA in Economics from Hendrix College (USA).

I worked as a Research Associate for the Institute of Public Administration (IPA), a recognised college of University College Dublin (UCD). In this position, I provided research, analytical, and statistical assistance as part of an Independent Secretariat for the Expert Commission on the Domestic Public Water Services. I also worked as a Research Economist for Indecon Economic Consultants in Dublin, Ireland, where I contributed to a number of economic impact assessments, policy reviews, and other assignments for various government departments and agencies.

Research

Publications

London calling? Agglomeration economies in literature since 1700. Journal of Urban Economics, 2019

This paper utilises a unique, purpose-built panel dataset on prominent authors in the UK and Ireland born 1700–1925 to estimate the productivity gains associated with agglomeration of an industry with few capital requirements and no apparent need to cluster geographically. I find the average author experiences productivity gains of 11.94% per annum when residing in London, the only major literary cluster – a gain not associated with living in any of the minor literary clusters. I find evidence of negative selection with respect to productivity, indicating the results are not driven by the self-selection of highly productive authors to London. I find heterogeneity of returns to living in London by birth cohort and Impact Index quartile (a measure of author quality) and that the cohorts who receive the greatest gains from locating in London are those for which there is the strongest evidence of negative selection with respect to productivity.

Other Research

Disentangling the effects of input sharing and knowledge spillovers in literary production. Working paper

This paper explores the ‘black box’ of agglomeration effects provides evidence of potential channels through which these gains occur in the context of literary production. In particular, this paper attempts to disentangle the effects of knowledge spillovers (proxied by characteristics of co-located social connections) and input sharing (proxied by characteristics of co-located but not socially-connected authors). This study utilises a unique dataset with information on 370 prominent authors in the UK and Ireland born 1700–1925. This paper constructs age-productivity profiles to determine the productivity gains associated with knowledge spillovers and input sharing. Individual fixed effects are used to control for spatial and group sorting. Input sharing of London-based authors affects own-productivity positively through output and negatively through quality. There is evidence knowledge spillovers when authors live elsewhere, as the output of co-located social connections has a positive effect on own-author productivity.

The origins of urban clusters in German literature (with Lukas Kuld) In progress

Large cities and clusters of workers are widely understood to be key factors in creative production. In this paper, we trace their empirical beginnings in the location choices made by German literary writers. We study yearly data on all 149 writers listed in the Encyclopædia Britannica as German and born between 1700 and 1899. This time frame includes major economic and political changes, such as the introduction of copyright. Writers mirror these changes in their mobility, location, and clustering choices. By the end of the 19th Century writers show a strong convergence into few large cities during early adulthood, which is not observed for earlier cohorts, and show typical modern life-cycle migration.

Making the list: Gender representation in literature (with Lukas Kuld) In progress

Looking at two hundred years of American literature, we compare female participation across different types of literature, success criteria, and data sources. Two key observations emerge. First, during the Twentieth Century, female writers hve a much higher representation in bestselling lists, at almost parity with male writers, than in literary cannons by present-day critics. Second, and similar to other socio-economic measures, we see a marked decrease in bestselling books by women during the mid-Twentieth Century. The same decrease is not observed for commercially less successful and for critically acclaimed publications. This might indicate an increasingly difficult market for female writers but also represents a convergence of gender roles for commercially and critically successful publications. In addition, by including a large range of data sources and metrics, we should how methodological choices can affect estimates on gender representation in the arts.

Toni Morrison and American literature: Determinants of female literary creativity (with Christiane Hellmanzik and Lukas Kuld) In progress

This paper investigates differences between female and male productivity in literary production using a novel dataset of eminent American writers of the 18th, 19th, and 20th centuries. Using yearly data on publications, critical acclaim information, as well as market success data and residency of 472 American writers, we show that differences between male and female literary productivity were not as large as expected and that career profiles over time are relatively similar when controlling for other factors. This is different from what we observe in other occupations, historically and contemporaneously. Moreover, we use information on trading laws in the US and exploit variation between states to test whether control and ownership over their works impacted female literary careers. Preliminary evidence suggests that this is indeed the case. One explanation is that economic incentives granted through legal autonomy spur women on to engage in creative activity.

Teaching Interests

Urban Economics

Course level: Bachelor or masters

Course description: In this course, we will pose and answer urban economics questions, such as why do cities exist? Why firms cluster? What determines city size and urban growth? We will then use economic theory to analyse the location choice of individuals and firms and to analyse the geography of education, employment, housing, transportation, poverty, and crime.

Core teaching elements: Lectures and exam (bachelor-level) or research papers with presentations (master-level)

Recommended reading:

  • Urban Economics by Arthur O'Sullivan
  • Lectures on Urban Economics by Jan K. Brueckner
  • The Triumph of the City by Edward Glaeser
  • Handbook of Urban and Regional Economics edited by Gilles Duranton, Vernon Henderson, and William Strange and other academic journal articles
  • Relevant news articles, podcasts and other sources

Economics of European Integration

Course level: Bachelor or masters

Course description: This course covers the microeconomics and macroeconomics the EU integration process from the post-war period to present day. We will discuss the phases of trade integration leading to the establishment of a single market, the geography of inequality and regional disparities and patterns of migration, the evolution of CAP and EU Regional Policy, theory of optimal currency areas, evolution of monetary policy and the euro, and the impact of the financial crisis, Brexit, and COVID-19.

Core teaching elements: Lectures, research paper with presentation

Recommended reading:

  • The Economics of European Integration by Charles Wyplosz and Richard E. Baldwin

Economics of Art and Culture

Course level: Masters

Course description: In this course, students will learn how to use tools of economics to interpret and understand cultural phenomena, identify cultural agents and their activities, and the understand relationship between culture and economics particularly in the context of the \textit{creative economy}. This course will cover topics such as: agents and pricing, demand for culture, the economics of creativity, cultural heritage and tourism, cultural policy (public funding of arts and culture, the economic impact of the arts), the impact of digitisation of cultural goods.

Core teaching elements: Lectures, research paper with presentation

Recommended reading:

  • Economics of Culture by David Throsby
  • Advanced Introduction to Cultural Economics by Ruth Towse
  • Cultural Economics by Christiane Hellmanzik
  • Handbook of Cultural Economics by edited by Ruth Towse and Trilce Navarrete Hernández
  • Rockonomics: A Backstage Tour of What the Music Industry Can Teach Us About Economics and Life by Alan Krueger

Economic History of Knowledge and Ideas

Course level: PhD

Course description: A number of innovations and circumstances throughout history have had major influence on the stock of knowledge, idea creation, human capital formation, and innovation. This course examines economic research about these phenomena, such as the introduction of the printing press, the Age of Enlightenment, the introduction of copyright, and conflict. Through this course, students will learn how to conduct economic history research, how to address various challenges in collecting historical data, and will understand various identification strategies used in historical contexts.

Core teaching elements: Lectures on research papers, referee report

Recommended reading:

  • Cagé, J., and Rueda, V. 2016. "The Long-Term Effects of the Printing Press in Sub-Saharan Africa." American Economic Journal: Applied Economics, 8 (3): 69-99.

Selected Course Descriptions

Economics of Migration

I lectured the Economics of Migration seminar offered by the Chair of Urban, Regional, and International Economics at TU Dortmund in Autumn 2018 and Autumn 2019. This seminar was taught to 25-30 final year bachelor students and masters students in the Department of Economics and the Department of Spatial Planning. The seminar was also open to students from several other faculties, such as industrial engineering and computer science.

The aim of this seminar was to explore the causes and effects of migration from a microeconomic and macroeconomic perspective and evaluate current economic policy debates from a scientific perspective. The Economics of Migration seminar lectures presented a series of economic research papers that broadly examine the following questions:

  • Who migrates?
  • How do immigrants do in the labour market relative to natives?
  • What impact do immigrants have on natives (wages, innovation)?

The rest of the seminar was designed to promote the discussion of scientific issues, active student participation in discussions, and intensive self-study. Students were required to work in groups to select a research topic related to migration, conduct an empirical analysis, and present their findings through an oral presentation and written report.

Academic Writing

I lectured the Academic Writing course offered by the Chair of Urban, Regional, and International Economics at TU Dortmund in Spring 2019. This course is taught to a small group of no more than 20 students. The course was open to students participating in the SPRING Masters Programme offered by the Department of Spatial Planning, and it was also open to bachelor students participating in the Digitalisation seminar (offered at the URI chair) who needed to fulfil their Modul 11: Wissenschafliches Arbeiten requirement.

During this course, students learn how to:

  • Structure ideas in academic writing: academic writing as ‘storytelling’
  • Understand the various types of writing and how this affects style, vocabulary and content
  • Discuss research in appropriate language for various audiences (scholarly and general)
  • Critique academic writing and how to respond to academic criticism (providing suggestions, accepting suggestions, defending decisions)
  • Present qualitative and quantitative analyses in a way that is meaningful and accessible
  • Writing concisely, accurately, and accessibly to communicate complex ideas

Relevant high-quality sources on academic citations/referencing, writing style guides, guides to presenting statistics, etc. are provided.

Recommended reading and other material:

  • Online Writing Lab at Purdue University
  • The Introduction Formula by Keith Head
  • The Conclusion Formula by Marc F. Bellemare
  • How to give an applied micro talk by Jesse M. Shapiro
  • Practical tips for writing and publishing applied economics papers by Timothy Beatty and Jay P. Shimshack
  • UNECE "Making Data Meaningful" Series
  • The Craft of Writing Effectively by Larry McEnerney

Mathematics and Statistics

I lectured the Mathematics section of the Mathematics and Statistics module offered by the Department of Economics in Trinity College Dublin in Autumn 2013, Autumn 2014, and Spring 2016. The module was taught to 375-400 first year bachelor students studying business, economics, and other social sciences in the School of Social Sciences and Philosophy. The 2 weekly lectures were supplemented with weekly tutorials (~20 students per tutorial) taught by 4-5 teaching assistants.

The content of the mathematics section of the module included:

  • Linear and quadratic functions
  • Indices and logarithms
  • Matrices
  • Differentiation
  • Optimisation of functions of one variable
  • Partial differentiation
  • Constrained and unconstrained optimisation of functions of several variables
  • Basic integration

The main text for this course was Mathematics for Economics and Business by Ian Jacques (Pearson). This textboox comes with a variety of online resources, including quizzes, that were used throughout the module.
