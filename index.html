Research

Research Overview

I am an empirical economist with research interests in urban economics, labour economics, economic history, and cultural economics. My research has touched on several subfields of economics, including migration, agglomeration of economic activity, and economic history of the arts. I use novel, historical datasets to study the migration and geographic clustering of creative workers and to identify agglomeration effects associated with artistic clusters. This work has involved extensive data collection, including the collection of automatically extracted data from structured data sources and the manual transcription of richer biographical information from unstructured data into a structured database.

My current research addresses the rise and decline of literary clusters, the migration of authors, patterns of agglomeration and co-agglomeration in creative industries, market forces in the development of literary clusters, individual life-cycle productivity of authors, and gender and racial gaps in authorship. I utilises data on individual authors spanning more than 200 years, allowing me the understand how historical events impact the life-cycle productivity of individual innovative agents. My research indicates that writers were extremely mobile relative to the general population, and that birth location and death location play a marginal role in lifetime productivity. Thus, the use of detailed lifetime information provides important insights into the circumstances of idea creation for those engaged in innovative and creative industries.

This work takes on increasing relevance in the current environment where traditional channels enabling knowledge spillovers, such as repeated face-to-face interaction, are limited. In the current situation, traditional literary salons cannot operate and artists are forced to explore new avenues for collaboration. Historical research on the impact of co-location and barriers to social interaction, such as war or the 1918 Flu Pandemic, on productivity may provide insights into how the current pandemic will impact creative and innovative production.

Publications

London calling? Agglomeration economies in literature since 1700. Journal of Urban Economics, 2019

This paper utilises a unique, purpose-built panel dataset on prominent authors in the UK and Ireland born 1700–1925 to estimate the productivity gains associated with agglomeration of an industry with few capital requirements and no apparent need to cluster geographically. I find the average author experiences productivity gains of 11.94% per annum when residing in London, the only major literary cluster – a gain not associated with living in any of the minor literary clusters. I find evidence of negative selection with respect to productivity, indicating the results are not driven by the self-selection of highly productive authors to London. I find heterogeneity of returns to living in London by birth cohort and Impact Index quartile (a measure of author quality) and that the cohorts who receive the greatest gains from locating in London are those for which there is the strongest evidence of negative selection with respect to productivity.

Other Research

Localisation effects in literature: Peer quantity, quality, or productivity? Working Paper

This paper utilises a unique dataset on 369 prominent authors in the UK and Ireland from 1725-1975 to provides evidence of on localisation effects (the number of authors in a given location) and urbanisation effects (the general urban environment). This paper also tests whether localisation effects occur through a quality channel (the quality of co-located authors), a quantity channel (the number of co-located authors), or a productivity channel (the output of co-located authors). It also uses data on author social connections to disentangle the effects of co-located social connections and the effects of other authors living in the same city. The results suggests that the localisation of authors in London results in a `crowding out' effects, while London urbanisation effects have a positive impact on own-productivity. The localisation effects occur primarily through quality and productivity channels, and these effects occur through the general clustering of authors and not through co-located social connections. There is also evidence that authors benefit most from localisation effects when they are under the age of 40 and thus in the early stages of their career.

Manhattan Transfer: Agglomeration and productivity in American literary production (with Lukas Kuld and Christiane Hellmanzik) In progress

New York City has dominated American literary production for almost 150 years. In this paper, we show strong agglomeration effects for authors residing in New York City during the industry's infancy before World War II. The effect declines with industry maturity and technological progress in communication and printing. We combine novel yearly data on publications and residency of 471 eminent authors with US census data to provide a complete picture of industry concentration and agglomeration effects since 1850. Taking relocation of working-age authors to New York City as an event study, we see an immediate increase in publications after arriving. However, the publication frequency of novels and nowadays successful works rises only gradually over the ten years following arrival in NYC.

The origins of urban clusters in German literature (with Lukas Kuld) In progress

Large cities and clusters of workers are widely understood to be key factors in creative production. In this paper, we trace their empirical beginnings in the location choices made by German literary writers. We study yearly data on all 149 writers listed in the Encyclopædia Britannica as German and born between 1700 and 1899. This time frame includes major economic and political changes, such as the introduction of copyright. Writers mirror these changes in their mobility, location, and clustering choices. By the end of the 19th Century writers show a strong convergence into few large cities during early adulthood, which is not observed for earlier cohorts, and show typical modern life-cycle migration.

Making the list: Gender representation in literature (with Lukas Kuld) In progress

Looking at two hundred years of American literature, we compare female participation across different types of literature, success criteria, and data sources. Two key observations emerge. First, during the Twentieth Century, female writers hve a much higher representation in bestselling lists, at almost parity with male writers, than in literary cannons by present-day critics. Second, and similar to other socio-economic measures, we see a marked decrease in bestselling books by women during the mid-Twentieth Century. The same decrease is not observed for commercially less successful and for critically acclaimed publications. This might indicate an increasingly difficult market for female writers but also represents a convergence of gender roles for commercially and critically successful publications. In addition, by including a large range of data sources and metrics, we should how methodological choices can affect estimates on gender representation in the arts.

Toni Morrison and American literature: Determinants of female literary creativity (with Christiane Hellmanzik and Lukas Kuld) In progress

This paper investigates differences between female and male productivity in literary production using a novel dataset of eminent American writers of the 18th, 19th, and 20th centuries. Using yearly data on publications, critical acclaim information, as well as market success data and residency of 472 American writers, we show that differences between male and female literary productivity were not as large as expected and that career profiles over time are relatively similar when controlling for other factors. This is different from what we observe in other occupations, historically and contemporaneously. Moreover, we use information on trading laws in the US and exploit variation between states to test whether control and ownership over their works impacted female literary careers. Preliminary evidence suggests that this is indeed the case. One explanation is that economic incentives granted through legal autonomy spur women on to engage in creative activity.

Economics of Migration

I lectured the Economics of Migration seminar offered by the Chair of Urban, Regional, and International Economics at TU Dortmund in Autumn 2018 and Autumn 2019. This seminar was taught to 25-30 final year bachelor students and masters students in the Department of Economics and the Department of Spatial Planning. The seminar was also open to students from several other faculties, such as industrial engineering and computer science.

The aim of this seminar was to explore the causes and effects of migration from a microeconomic and macroeconomic perspective and evaluate current economic policy debates from a scientific perspective. The Economics of Migration seminar lectures presented a series of economic research papers that broadly examine the following questions:

  • Who migrates?
  • How do immigrants do in the labour market relative to natives?
  • What impact do immigrants have on natives (wages, innovation)?

The rest of the seminar was designed to promote the discussion of scientific issues, active student participation in discussions, and intensive self-study. Students were required to work in groups to select a research topic related to migration, conduct an empirical analysis, and present their findings through an oral presentation and written report.

Human Capital and the Knowledge Economy

I was a teaching assistant for the Human Capital and the Knowledge Economy course offered by the Chair of Urban, Regional, and International Economics at TU Dormtund in Spring 2019 and Spring 2020. This course is normally taught to approx. 60-80 bachelor students in the Department of Economics.

This course discusses the role of knowledge and education in the careers of individuals and for the economy as a whole. The lectures introduced the students to theoretical models and empirical evidence, while the tutorials provided practice exercises for the theoretical models and applied econometrics examples. For example, the practice exercises included questions on the Becker and Spence models, questions on how various empirical approaches address the issue of ability bias, and questions on how to interpret the output of Mincer earnings regressions.

Economics of Brexit

I lectured the Economics of Brexit seminar offered by the Chair of Urban, Regional, and International Economics at TU Dortmund in Spring 2020. This seminar was taught to approx. 20 final year bachelor students and masters students in the Department of Economics and the Department of Spatial Planning. The seminar was also open to students from several other faculties, such as industrial engineering and computer science.

This seminar aims to build on students’ prior knowledge of international trade, factor market integration, tariff policy, and regional competition and industrial policy. Students will apply this knowledge to a practical, real-world example – Brexit. In this setting, students will:

  • Gain a deeper understanding of complex trade interdependencies between EU and other countries;
  • Learn about the economic framework of decision-making within the EU, particularly with respect to how EU trade policies are set within the EU and between EU and other countries;
  • Explore the European Union’s legal framework and legislative process with respect to developing trade policies;
  • Learn how to evaluate current economic policy debates by synthesizing country-specific trade policy and data and producing a policy report from that country’s perspective.

The first half of the course involves lectures on the history of European economic integration. These lectures provide an overview of the history of the European Union, EU institutions and decision-making processes before discussing the microeconomics and macroeconomics of European integration. Topics covered include: preferential trade liberalisation, customs union vs free trade agreement, types of barriers to trade, allocation and growth effects of trade liberalisation, effect of trade liberalisation on labour markets and migration, exchange rates, optimum currency areas, monetary integration, fiscal policy, and the effect of the global financial crisis. This seminar discussed EU policies such as Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), EU structural funds and cohesion policy, EU competition and state aid, and the Stability and Growth Pact.

In the second week, students are allocated into groups and assigned an EU member state, and the students are required to research that country's economic relationship with the UK and how it will likely be affected by Brexit. During the second half of the course, the students present their country's position to the class and engage in simulated Brexit negotiations. The assessments include two shorter summary reports submitted before and after the negotiation process and a longer research paper submitted at the end of the course.

Reading list:

  • Primary textbook: Baldwin and Wyplosx (2020) "The Economics of European Integration"
  • Secondary textbook: Hix and Høyland (2011). "The Political System of the European Union"

Academic Writing

I lectured the Academic Writing course offered by the Chair of Urban, Regional, and International Economics at TU Dortmund in Spring 2019. This course is taught to a small group of no more than 20 students. The course was open to students participating in the SPRING Masters Programme offered by the Department of Spatial Planning, and it was also open to bachelor students participating in the Digitalisation seminar (offered at the URI chair) who needed to fulfil their Modul 11: Wissenschafliches Arbeiten requirement.

During this course, students learn how to:

  • Structure ideas in academic writing: academic writing as ‘storytelling’
  • Understand the various types of writing and how this affects style, vocabulary and content
  • Discuss research in appropriate language for various audiences (scholarly and general)
  • Critique academic writing and how to respond to academic criticism (providing suggestions, accepting suggestions, defending decisions)
  • Present qualitative and quantitative analyses in a way that is meaningful and accessible
  • Writing concisely, accurately, and accessibly to communicate complex ideas

Relevant high-quality sources on academic citations/referencing, writing style guides, guides to presenting statistics, etc. are provided.

Recommended reading and other material:

  • Online Writing Lab at Purdue University
  • The Introduction Formula by Keith Head
  • The Conclusion Formula by Marc F. Bellemare
  • How to give an applied micro talk by Jesse M. Shapiro
  • Practical tips for writing and publishing applied economics papers by Timothy Beatty and Jay P. Shimshack
  • UNECE "Making Data Meaningful" Series
  • The Craft of Writing Effectively by Larry McEnerney

Mathematics and Statistics

I lectured the Mathematics section of the Mathematics and Statistics module offered by the Department of Economics in Trinity College Dublin in Autumn 2013, Autumn 2014, and Spring 2016. The module was taught to 375-400 first year bachelor students studying business, economics, and other social sciences in the School of Social Sciences and Philosophy. The 2 weekly lectures were supplemented with weekly tutorials (~20 students per tutorial) taught by 4-5 teaching assistants.

The content of the mathematics section of the module included:

  • Linear and quadratic functions
  • Indices and logarithms
  • Matrices
  • Differentiation
  • Optimisation of functions of one variable
  • Partial differentiation
  • Constrained and unconstrained optimisation of functions of several variables
  • Basic integration

The main text for this course was Mathematics for Economics and Business by Ian Jacques (Pearson). This textboox comes with a variety of online resources, including quizzes, that were used throughout the module.
